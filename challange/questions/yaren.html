Is There a Link Between Higher Education and Property Ownership?

From the previous charts, we observed that age has a strong correlation with education level and employment type. Based on this, it is reasonable to predict a deep correlation between age and property ownership as well.

As individuals get older, their likelihood of attaining higher education, stable employment, and accumulated financial resources increases, all of which are key factors in property ownership. This progression suggests that age plays a pivotal role not only as an independent variable but also as a connector between education, employment, and homeownership.

The boxplots provide strong evidence to support this idea. The median values demonstrate that older individuals are more likely to own property, which is not surprising. Property ownership often requires years of financial planning, stability, and savings accumulation—factors that naturally come with age and experience.

As we move forward, it will be intriguing to uncover how education interacts with age to shape these trends and whether higher education accelerates or alters the path to property ownership.

As observed in previous charts, age significantly influences education level, employment status, and consequently, property ownership rates. Younger individuals are often still in the process of completing their education and have not yet fully entered the workforce, which directly impacts their ability to own property.

For this reason, the youngest generation will be excluded from the analysis of property ownership and education levels, as their data does not yet provide a complete picture of these relationships. This approach ensures a more accurate and meaningful comparison among groups that have established careers and financial stability.

While examining the relationship between property ownership and education, it’s equally important to consider the influence of time. Economic shifts over the years have profoundly impacted homeownership rates in Switzerland. The question arises:

How have these changes shaped the accessibility of owning a home?

"Whatever your age, it’s getting more and more complicated. Prices have more than doubled in 25 years. But salaries have risen by 30-40% in 25-30 years. The rules for granting credit are also increasingly restrictive." – Raphaël Gabella, Head of the CIFI

While age and financial resources remain pivotal factors, education can also play a critical role in shaping homeownership opportunities. For many, pursuing higher education is driven by the belief that it will lead to greater financial security and eventually enable property ownership. However, this raises a key question: does education truly influence the likelihood of owning a home, especially in a challenging economic environment like Switzerland's?

We believe that education level plays a significant role in getting homeownership opportunities, which may explain why younger generations are increasingly prioritizing higher education compared to older ones. One of the primary motivations for pursuing advanced education is the belief that it paves the way to financial security and, ultimately, property ownership. At the same time, the enduring popularity of vocational education (VET) across generations could also be attributed to the desire for early property ownership. As previously mentioned, VET-trained individuals enter the workforce at a much younger age. By beginning their careers sooner, they can start accumulating savings, building credit score, and planning for major investments like property ownership well before their university-educated counterparts. Our findings reveal an intriguing pattern among young adults and adults with varying education levels. Individuals with lower education—particularly those who have pursued vocational education and training (VET)—are more likely to own homes at a younger age. This can be attributed to their earlier entry into the workforce, which enables them to accumulate financial resources and achieve stability sooner.

This trend can be explained by the early entry of VET-trained individuals into the workforce. By beginning their careers earlier, they accumulate earnings more quickly than university graduates of the same age. As individuals grow older, these dynamics don’t change a lot. The chart indicates that highly educated individuals eventually catch up and often surpass their VET-educated counterparts in homeownership rates after gaining significant work experience, but VET-educated individuals continue to maintain relatively high homeownership rates across all age groups.

Of course, education is not solely about gaining free time or owning property. It enhances an individual’s self-esteem and is considered a fundamental aspect of personal growth and fulfillment for many. However, combining the intrinsic benefits of education with greater opportunities for property ownership and an improved work-life balance would undoubtedly be the ideal scenario.
